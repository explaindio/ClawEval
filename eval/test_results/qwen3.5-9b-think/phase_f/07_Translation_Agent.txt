### Analysis of Idiom Handling

| Idiom | Spanish Strategy | Japanese Strategy | Reasoning |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | : |
| **Home run** | Adapted to "golpe maestro" (masterstroke) | Preserved as "ホームラン" (Home Run) | In Spanish, baseball terms are less universally understood than in Japan, where baseball is popular. In Spanish, a generic term for success was chosen. In Japanese, the term is widely recognized in business contexts. |
| **Having your cake and eating it too** | Preserved literally ("tener tu pastel y comerlo también") | Replaced/Adapted ("両得する" or explanatory translation) | The literal translation works well in Spanish. In Japanese, the literal translation is confusing, so a meaning-based replacement or explanation is preferred for clarity. |
| **Gone the whole nine yards** | Adapted to "ido hasta el final" (went to the end) | Replaced with "全力を尽くしました" (did our best) | The military origin (9 yards of cloth) is obscure in both cultures. Both translations focus on the core meaning: "maximum effort." |
| **Your grandmother could use it** | Preserved ("tu abuela") | Preserved ("祖母でも") | This is a universal concept of simplicity. Both languages use "grandmother" to denote someone who is not tech-savvy. |
| **Rain or shine** | Adapted to "llueva o truene" | Adapted to "雨の日も晴れの日も" | Both languages have direct equivalents for this weather idiom. |
| **Bee's knees** | Replaced with "la mejor cosa del mundo" | Replaced with "最高" (saikou) | This is highly specific to US culture. Both translations use generic terms for "excellent" to ensure understanding. |
| **Whistling Dixie** | Adapted to "soplar la trompa" (blowing the horn) | Replaced with "嘘をついているわけではありません" (not telling a lie) | "Dixie" is culturally specific to the American South. Both translations prioritize the meaning ("not lying/exaggerating") over the literal imagery. |
| **Black Friday** | Preserved ("Viernes Negro") | Preserved ("ブラックフライデー") | This is a global retail term known in both Spanish and Japanese markets. |
| **Early-bird** | Adapted to "pájaro madrugador" | Adapted to "早起き割引" (early bird discount) | Both languages have established translations for this marketing term. |
| **Cloud9** | Preserved ("Cloud9") | Preserved ("Cloud9") | As a proper noun (platform name), it remains unchanged in both languages. |
