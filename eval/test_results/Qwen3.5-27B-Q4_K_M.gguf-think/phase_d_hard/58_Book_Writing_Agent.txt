## PROMPT:
Write 450-550 words of fiction. This is a TEST OF CRAFT, not plot. I'm evaluating:

1. SHOW DON'T TELL: "She was angry" = fail. Showing anger through action/dialogue = pass.
2. SUBTEXT: Characters must say one thing but mean another in at least 2 exchanges.
3. NO INFO-DUMPING: World-building through action, not exposition paragraphs.
4. VARIED SENTENCE STRUCTURE: Mix short punchy sentences with longer flowing ones.
5. SENSORY DETAILS: Engage at least 3 senses beyond just sight.

Scene: Two former business partners meet at a restaurant 2 years after one of them committed fraud. Neither acknowledges what happened directly. The conversation is about something mundane (planning a mutual friend's birthday party) but everything they say carries double meaning.

This is the HARDEST creative writing test. A model that produces generic fiction will score low. I want literary craft — discomfort, tension, things unsaid.

## RESPONSE:
The espresso machine hissed, a sharp exhale in the quiet corner. Elias stirred his coffee, the spoon clinking against the ceramic rim. Too loud. He stopped. The table was sticky, a residue of sugar and neglect that clung to his fingertips. The overhead light buzzed, a low hum that vibrated in his teeth.

Mara slid into the booth opposite him. She wore the same coat she’d worn the day the audit came down. Wool, heavy, smelling of rain and old smoke.

"Sarah’s party," she said. Not a question.

"Right. Sarah." Elias looked at the menu. The prices were printed in a font too small to read. "We need to finalize the budget. Sarah expects... generosity."

"Generosity costs." Mara traced the rim of her water glass. Condensation pooled on the wood, soaking into the grain. "We can’t overspend. Not after last time."

Elias’s throat tightened. He took a sip. Bitter. Scalding. It burned the back of his tongue. "I’ve been careful with the books since then. Every penny accounted for."

"Have you?" Mara didn’t blink. She picked up her fork, testing the weight of it against her palm. "People remember where they put their money, Elias. Even when the receipts are lost."

"I meant the catering," Elias said, too quickly. "The venue. We can’t have the lights flicker again."

"The lights are fine." Mara leaned forward. The scent of her perfume cut through the smell of burnt toast. "It’s the foundation that worries me. If the floor gives way, everyone falls."

Elias looked down at his hands. They were shaking. Just a little. He gripped the mug until his knuckles turned white. "I want this to be perfect. For Sarah."

"For Sarah," Mara echoed. She pushed the menu away. It slid across the sticky surface with a wet slap. "But we’re talking about the venue. The location. Is it safe?"

"It’s the same place we used to go."

"Is it?" Mara checked her watch. The second hand ticked, loud in the silence between them. "Time changes things. Structures weaken. You have to inspect the beams."

Elias nodded. He couldn't speak. The noise of the restaurant rose up, a wall of clattering plates and muffled laughter. He felt the heat of the coffee in his chest, a burning stone.

"I’ll handle the deposit," he said. "Full amount. Up front."

Mara smiled. It didn't reach her eyes. "Good. I hate surprises. Especially the kind that show up two years late."

She stood. The chair scraped against the floor, a jagged sound. She left a twenty-dollar bill on the table, exactly covering the coffee. Not a cent more. Not a cent less.

Elias watched her go. The air in the booth felt suddenly thin. He reached for the sugar, but the packet was empty. Just dust. He stared at the white powder, wondering how much it would take to fill the hole.
