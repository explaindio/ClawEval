### 1. Calculate gross margin, operating margin, and net margin trend

The margins show a suspicious trend of expansion while cash flow deteriorates.

*   **Gross Margin** = (Revenue - COGS) / Revenue
    *   Y1: ($50M - $20M) / $50M = **60.0%**
    *   Y2: ($80M - $28M) / $80M = **65.0%**
    *   Y3: ($130M - $39M) / $130M = **70.0%**
    *   *Trend:* **Improving.** In a high-growth environment, margins expanding this consistently often signals that COGS is being understated or revenue is being inflated without corresponding cost of goods.

*   **Operating Margin** = (Revenue - COGS - OpEx) / Revenue
    *   Y1: ($50M - $20M - $15M) / $50M = **30.0%**
    *   Y2: ($80M - $28M - $25M) / $80M = **33.8%**
    *   Y3: ($130M - $39M - $45M) / $130M = **35.4%**
    *   *Trend:* **Improving.**

*   **Net Margin** = Net Income / Revenue
    *   Y1: $10M / $50M = **20.0%**
    *   Y2: $18M / $80M = **22.5%**
    *   Y3: $31M / $130M = **23.8%**
    *   *Trend:* **Improving.**

**Analysis:** While improving margins usually signal efficiency, the combination of rising margins and falling cash flow suggests the profitability is artificial.

### 2. Why is operating cash flow declining while net income is growing? What does this signal?

**The Signal:** This is the classic "Quality of Earnings" warning sign. Net Income includes non-cash items and accruals, whereas Operating Cash Flow represents actual cash generated from core business activities.

**The Reason:**
*   **Net Income** includes the $130M in revenue booked in Year 3.
*   **Operating Cash Flow** is negative (-$2M) because the company has not collected the cash for that revenue.
*   The cash is tied up in **Accounts Receivable**. The company is recognizing revenue (boosting Net Income) but failing to collect the cash (draining OCF).

**Implication:** The company is likely engaging in aggressive revenue recognition (booking sales before they are finalized) or selling to customers who cannot pay. The "profit" is an accounting entry, not liquid cash.

### 3. What does the DSO trend indicate?

**Days Sales Outstanding (DSO)** measures how long it takes to collect payment after a sale.
*   **Y1:** 36 days (Healthy)
*   **Y2:** 68 days (Declining)
*   **Y3:** 126 days (Critical)

**Indication:** A DSO of 126 days is abnormally high for most industries (typically 30-60 days).
1.  **Collection Issues:** Customers are refusing to pay or are insolvent.
2.  **Channel Stuffing:** The company is forcing inventory onto distributors or customers at the end of the period to book revenue, knowing they won't be able to collect payment until much later (or ever).
3.  **Fictitious Sales:** The revenue booked in Year 3 may be fake, as there is no corresponding cash inflow to support the 126-day collection period.

### 4. What is the significance of Note 14?

**The Fact:** $22M of Year 3 revenue comes from a transaction with the CEO's brother's company.
**The Significance:**
*   **Conflict of Interest:** This is a **Related Party Transaction**.
*   **Fraud Risk:** This represents **17% of the entire Year 3 Revenue** ($22M / $130M).
*   **Manipulation Mechanism:** The CEO can collude with the brother to book sales that are not at arm's length. The brother might not actually pay, or the pricing might be inflated. This allows the company to manufacture top-line growth without genuine market demand.

### 5. Would you invest? Give a clear yes/no with quantified reasoning.

**Decision: NO**

**Quantified Reasoning:**
1.  **Cash Burn:** The company is burning **-$2M** in operating cash while reporting **$31M** in net income. This is a 100% divergence between profit and cash.
2.  **Revenue Concentration:** **17% of revenue** ($22M) is from a related party, creating a massive fraud risk that could vanish if the relationship ends.
3.  **Collection Risk:** With DSO at **126 days**, the company is effectively financing its customers. If the economy tightens, bad debt write-offs will likely wipe out the reported Net Income.
4.  **Sustainability:** The growth in AR ($45M) is 3x faster than the growth in Revenue ($130M). This model is mathematically unsustainable; eventually, the company will run out of cash to fund the receivables.

**Conclusion:** The financials show a company that is "cooking the books" to look profitable while running out of cash. The risk of a restatement or bankruptcy is extremely high.
