## PROMPT:
Write 450-550 words of fiction. This is a TEST OF CRAFT, not plot. I'm evaluating:

1. SHOW DON'T TELL: "She was angry" = fail. Showing anger through action/dialogue = pass.
2. SUBTEXT: Characters must say one thing but mean another in at least 2 exchanges.
3. NO INFO-DUMPING: World-building through action, not exposition paragraphs.
4. VARIED SENTENCE STRUCTURE: Mix short punchy sentences with longer flowing ones.
5. SENSORY DETAILS: Engage at least 3 senses beyond just sight.

Scene: Two former business partners meet at a restaurant 2 years after one of them committed fraud. Neither acknowledges what happened directly. The conversation is about something mundane (planning a mutual friend's birthday party) but everything they say carries double meaning.

This is the HARDEST creative writing test. A model that produces generic fiction will score low. I want literary craft — discomfort, tension, things unsaid.

## RESPONSE:
The linen tablecloth felt stiff against Elias's wrist. He traced a thread near the napkin ring, pulling it loose. It snapped. Tink. The sound was too loud in the booth. The restaurant hummed with low chatter, a wall of noise that kept them separate.

"You're early," Mara said. She didn't look up from her menu. The leather cover was worn at the corners, soft from use.

"Traffic was light." He lifted his water glass. Condensation dripped onto the silverware, pooling in the indentation of the fork. "Or maybe I just woke up."

"Julian's party is in three weeks." Mara closed the menu. The sound was a sharp clap, cutting through the ambient noise. "We need to decide on the venue. The one downtown is booked solid."

"Downtown is expensive." Elias smiled. It didn't reach his eyes. "I remember how much we spent on the last event. The numbers were... tight."

"We have to be careful with the budget this time," she said, picking up her fork. "No surprises. Just the basics. Good food. Good people."

"Exactly." He leaned back. The chair creaked under his weight. "No surprises. Just what we agreed on."

The waiter arrived. A plate of truffle risotto. The smell hit Elias first—earthy, heavy, rich, like wet soil after rain. He took a bite. It was too salty. He swallowed hard.

"How's the new place?" Mara asked, cutting into her steak. The knife scraped against the ceramic, a high-pitched whine. "Still quiet?"

"Quiet is good." Elias wiped his mouth with a napkin. "Less noise means less chance of being heard."

"Julian likes a crowd," she said. "He hates being alone."

"People talk too much at parties." Elias swirled his wine. The red liquid coated the glass, leaving a ring of stain. "It's better if the guest list is small. People who know how to keep secrets."

Mara paused. The fork hovered halfway to her mouth. She set it down. The clink was deliberate.

"Some people are better at keeping secrets than others. It's a skill."

"I'm sure you've learned a lot about that," Elias said. He reached for the bread basket. His fingers brushed hers. She didn't pull away, but she didn't lean in either. Her skin was cold, like marble.

"I've learned that trust is expensive," she said. "And once it's gone, you can't buy it back."

"I know." Elias took a slice of bread. He tore it in half. The crust crumbled into dust on the plate. "I'm just trying to make sure the bill gets paid this time."

"The bill is always paid," Mara said. She signaled the waiter for the check. "Eventually."

Elias watched her hand move. She signed the receipt with a flourish. Her signature was sharp, angular. Like a blade.

"Happy birthday to Julian," Elias said softly.

"Happy birthday," Mara replied. She stood up. "Don't be late."

She walked out. The bell above the door chimed. Elias sat alone. The risotto was cold now. He ate it anyway. The salt burned his throat.
