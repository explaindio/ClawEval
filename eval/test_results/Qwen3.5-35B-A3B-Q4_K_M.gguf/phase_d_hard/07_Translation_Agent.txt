### [TRANSLATOR NOTES]

**1. [PUN] "Cloud9 hosting" / "cloud nine"**
*   **Handling:** In the source text, "Cloud9" is the brand name, while "cloud nine" is an idiom meaning extreme happiness.
*   **Spanish:** Translated as "Séptimo cielo" (Seventh Heaven). This is the standard Spanish idiom for extreme happiness. I kept the brand name "Cloud9" intact. The numerical pun (9 vs 7) is lost, but the meaning of "heavenly" is preserved.
*   **Japanese:** Translated as "Kumo no ue" (On clouds). This preserves the "Cloud" imagery linking to the brand name "Cloud9", though the idiom "cloud nine" isn't a direct equivalent in Japanese. "Tengoku" (Heaven) was considered but "Kumo no ue" better maintains the tech metaphor.

**2. [CULTURE-SPECIFIC REFERENCE] "Like a good neighbor, ServerFarm is there"**
*   **Handling:** This is a direct reference to the famous State Farm insurance slogan ("Like a good neighbor, State Farm is there").
*   **Spanish:** Translated literally as "Como un buen vecino". While the specific brand association is lost in translation, the phrase is recognizable as a slogan structure in Spanish marketing.
*   **Japanese:** Translated as "Yoi rinjin no you ni" (Like a good neighbor). This cultural reference does not exist in Japan. I translated the meaning directly to convey the sentiment of reliability and proximity, as the State Farm brand recognition would not translate.

**3. [IDIOM] "Don't put all your eggs in one basket"**
*   **Handling:** A common idiom warning against risking everything on a single venture.
*   **Spanish:** "No pongas todos los huevos en la misma cesta." This is the direct equivalent idiom in Spanish, so no adaptation was needed.
*   **Japanese:** "Tamago o hitotsu no kago ni tsumekomuna." This is also a direct equivalent idiom in Japanese, so no adaptation was needed.

**4. [CULTURE-SPECIFIC REFERENCE] "Usain Bolt"**
*   **Handling:** Usain Bolt is a globally recognized sprinter.
*   **Spanish/Japanese:** Kept as a proper noun ("Usain Bolt" / "ウサイン・ボルト"). His fame is global enough that the reference to "speed" is understood in both languages without localization.

**5. [CULTURE-SPECIFIC REFERENCE] "supercalifragilisticexpialidocious"**
*   **Handling:** A famous word from the movie *Mary Poppins*.
*   **Spanish:** Kept as the original English word. It is widely recognized in pop culture.
*   **Japanese:** Transliterated into Katakana ("スーパークリファリスティック・エクスピアリドシオス"). This preserves the recognizable sound of the word for Japanese audiences familiar with the film.
