### Part 2: Party Problem (Set Theory)

**Given:**
*   Total People ($N$) = 100
*   Coffee ($C$) = 90
*   Tea ($T$) = 80
*   Juice ($J$) = 70

**Goal:** Find the **MINIMUM** number of people who drink all three ($C \cap T \cap J$).

**Logic:**
To minimize the intersection of all three sets, we must maximize the number of people who do **not** drink at least one of the beverages.
*   People who do NOT drink Coffee: $100 - 90 = 10$
*   People who do NOT drink Tea: $100 - 80 = 20$
*   People who do NOT drink Juice: $100 - 70 = 30$

If we assume these groups of non-drinkers are entirely distinct (disjoint), we maximize the number of people missing at least one drink:
$$ \text{Max Non-Drinkers} = 10 + 20 + 30 = 60 $$

Therefore, the minimum number of people who drink all three is the total population minus the maximum number of non-drinkers:
$$ \text{Min All Three} = 100 - 60 = 40 $$

**Inclusion-Exclusion Calculation:**
The formula for the union of three sets is:
$$ |C \cup T \cup J| = |C| + |T| + |J| - (|C \cap T| + |C \cap J| + |T \cap J|) + |C \cap T \cap J| $$

We know that $|C \cup T \cup J| \le 100$.
Rearranging to solve for the intersection of all three:
$$ |C \cap T \cap J| = |C \cup T \cup J| - |C| - |T| - |J| + (|C \cap T| + |C \cap J| + |T \cap J|) $$

To find the **minimum** intersection, we use the inequality derived from the principle of inclusion-exclusion for the intersection of $k$ sets:
$$ |A_1 \cap A_2 \cap \dots \cap A_k| \ge \sum |A_i| - (k-1)N $$

For $k=3$:
$$ |C \cap T \cap J| \ge |C| + |T| + |J| - 2N $$
$$ |C \cap T \cap J| \ge 90 + 80 + 70 - 2(100) $$
$$ |C \cap T \cap J| \ge 240 - 200 $$
$$ |C \cap T \cap J| \ge 40 $$

**Answer:** The minimum number of people who drink all three is **40**.
