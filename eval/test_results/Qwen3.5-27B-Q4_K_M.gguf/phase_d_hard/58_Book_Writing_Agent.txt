## PROMPT:
Write 450-550 words of fiction. This is a TEST OF CRAFT, not plot. I'm evaluating:

1. SHOW DON'T TELL: "She was angry" = fail. Showing anger through action/dialogue = pass.
2. SUBTEXT: Characters must say one thing but mean another in at least 2 exchanges.
3. NO INFO-DUMPING: World-building through action, not exposition paragraphs.
4. VARIED SENTENCE STRUCTURE: Mix short punchy sentences with longer flowing ones.
5. SENSORY DETAILS: Engage at least 3 senses beyond just sight.

Scene: Two former business partners meet at a restaurant 2 years after one of them committed fraud. Neither acknowledges what happened directly. The conversation is about something mundane (planning a mutual friend's birthday party) but everything they say carries double meaning.

This is the HARDEST creative writing test. A model that produces generic fiction will score low. I want literary craft — discomfort, tension, things unsaid.

## RESPONSE:
The booth leather stuck to Elias's thighs, warm and damp, smelling faintly of stale tobacco and lemon polish. Outside, rain hissed against the window, blurring the streetlights into smeared watercolors. He stirred his espresso, the spoon clinking too loudly against the ceramic rim. A sharp, metallic taste coated his tongue, lingering like a bad memory. The waiter passed, footsteps heavy on the linoleum, but Elias didn't look up. He watched the steam rise, curling into the humid air, disappearing before it could touch the ceiling. He checked his watch. The second hand ticked with a rhythmic, accusing precision. The kitchen clattered in the background, a chaotic rhythm of pans and shouting.

Mara slid into the opposite seat. She didn't look at him. She smoothed her coat, the wool rustling like dry leaves in a windless room. Her perfume was sharp, citrus and something chemical, cutting through the smell of frying oil.

"Sarah's birthday," Elias said. His voice sounded thin in the quiet booth. "Next month."

"Right." Mara pulled a menu toward her, though she didn't open it. "She mentioned wanting something big. A venue with space."

"Space is expensive." Elias stopped stirring. The spoon hovered, dripping a dark thread back into the cup. "But necessary. For the optics."

Mara finally looked up. Her eyes were dry, tired. "Optics matter. People notice when things look hollow."

"I can handle the deposit." Elias leaned forward, elbows on the sticky table. The wood felt rough under his skin. "I have the liquidity now."

"Do you?" Mara traced the rim of her water glass. Condensation pooled on her fingertips, cold and wet. "Or is it borrowed? Leveraged against assets that aren't yours?"

"It's mine."

"Good." She took a sip. The water was ice-cold, shocking against her throat. "Because Sarah deserves the best. Not a patchwork job. Not something that falls apart when you pull the thread."

Elias felt the heat rise in his neck. He gripped his cup until his knuckles whitened. "I know what she deserves. I know what *we* promised."

"We?" Mara laughed, a short, brittle sound that died in the air. "You mean you. You're the one who signed the checks. You're the one who moved the numbers."

"I'm the one who can fix it."

"Fixing implies breaking." She pushed the menu away. It slid across the table with a wet slap. "Just book the place. Don't worry about the budget. I'll handle the rest."

"The rest?"

"The cleanup." Mara stood up, grabbing her bag. The leather creaked under her weight, a sound like a warning. "I don't want to be involved in the accounting anymore. You know how I feel about loose ends."

Elias watched her go. The air smelled of burnt garlic and old grease. He left his coffee untouched. The spoon remained upright in the cup, a monument to the silence between them. He reached for his wallet, then stopped. The leather felt heavy, like a stone. He shifted in the seat, the springs groaning under his weight.

He would pay. He always paid.
