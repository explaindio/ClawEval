## PROMPT:
Write 450-550 words of fiction. This is a TEST OF CRAFT, not plot. I'm evaluating:

1. SHOW DON'T TELL: "She was angry" = fail. Showing anger through action/dialogue = pass.
2. SUBTEXT: Characters must say one thing but mean another in at least 2 exchanges.
3. NO INFO-DUMPING: World-building through action, not exposition paragraphs.
4. VARIED SENTENCE STRUCTURE: Mix short punchy sentences with longer flowing ones.
5. SENSORY DETAILS: Engage at least 3 senses beyond just sight.

Scene: Two former business partners meet at a restaurant 2 years after one of them committed fraud. Neither acknowledges what happened directly. The conversation is about something mundane (planning a mutual friend's birthday party) but everything they say carries double meaning.

This is the HARDEST creative writing test. A model that produces generic fiction will score low. I want literary craft — discomfort, tension, things unsaid.

## RESPONSE:
The chipped Formica of the booth felt cold under Leo’s elbow. Two years. Two years since Amelia had…reallocated funds. He hadn’t requested this meeting, hadn’t sought her out. Sarah, their shared, infuriatingly optimistic friend, had insisted. “You *have* to be involved in the planning, Leo. It’s not a party without both of you.”

Amelia arrived precisely on time, the scent of expensive jasmine clinging to her coat. She didn’t look at him directly, instead focusing on smoothing the pleats of her skirt. “Traffic was…predictable.” A small, tight smile. Predictable, like the inevitable fallout?

“Right,” Leo said, picking up the laminated menu. He didn’t need it. He’d eaten here a hundred times with Amelia, dissecting spreadsheets, dreaming of expansion. “Sarah mentioned a theme. Eighties?”

“She’s very enthusiastic about leg warmers.” Amelia’s voice was light, almost brittle. “Though I suggested something a little more…sophisticated. A cocktail party, perhaps? More our speed.”

Leo traced the rim of a water glass. *Our speed*. Before. “Sarah doesn’t *do* sophisticated. Remember the luau? The inflatable palm trees?” He remembered Amelia, laughing, meticulously tracking the budget for the ridiculous party, every penny accounted for. A budget she’d later…adjusted.

“That was…spirited.” Amelia’s fingers tapped a rhythm on the table, a quick, nervous beat. “I was thinking we could divide tasks. You always handled the music. I could take care of the venue.”

“Funny you say that.” Leo’s voice was deliberately flat. He hadn’t touched the music in two years. The silence stretched, punctuated only by the clatter of plates from a nearby table and the low hum of the refrigerator. He could taste the stale coffee lingering in the air, a familiar restaurant smell now tainted with something acrid. 

“I just meant,” Amelia said quickly, “I have more free time now. Things are…less demanding.” Less demanding than explaining where a quarter of a million dollars went.

“Right.” Leo finally looked up, meeting her gaze. Her eyes were carefully neutral. “Plenty of time to handle details. To *ensure* everything is correct.” He let the word hang, heavy. 

Amelia’s smile didn’t reach her eyes. “Of course. Precision is key. Especially with Sarah. She’d notice if the ice sculpture wasn’t *exactly* a unicorn.” 

He imagined the ice sculpture, melting slowly, a distorted, fragile form. Like their partnership. “She’d notice a lot of things.” He paused, then added, with a casualness he didn't feel, “I’m good with the playlist. Synth-pop, definitely. Some early Madonna.” 

“Excellent.” Amelia pulled out a small, leather-bound planner. “I’ve already started a spreadsheet. Just to keep things organized.” The planner was a deliberate show, a return to normalcy. A shield. 

Leo leaned back, the cold Formica pressing into his arm. The music in the restaurant shifted to a saccharine eighties ballad. He wondered if Amelia ever thought about the spreadsheets she’d falsified, the trust she’d broken. He doubted it. She was too busy planning a party, meticulously controlling the details, trying to rebuild a facade of order. And he was playing along, because Sarah had asked him to, and because sometimes, the quietest revenge was simply watching someone meticulously construct their own prison.
