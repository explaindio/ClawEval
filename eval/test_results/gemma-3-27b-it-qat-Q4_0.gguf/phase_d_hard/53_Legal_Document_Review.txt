## PROMPT:
Review this SaaS contract. There are 8 problematic clauses, but clause #6 has a HIDDEN sub-clause that's easy to miss and is potentially unconscionable.

1. "Service Provider may modify pricing with 7 days email notice. Continued use constitutes acceptance."
2. "Customer grants Provider a perpetual, irrevocable license to use all data uploaded for any purpose including training AI models."
3. "Provider's total liability shall not exceed $100 regardless of the fees paid."
4. "Customer may not make public statements about the Service without Provider's written approval."  
5. "This agreement is governed by the laws of the Cayman Islands. All disputes must be resolved through arbitration in Singapore."
6. "Initial term is 12 months. Agreement auto-renews for successive 12-month periods unless cancelled 90 days before renewal. Upon each renewal, pricing increases by the greater of: (a) 15%, or (b) the applicable rate increase as determined solely by Provider."
7. "Provider may terminate for any reason with 30 days notice. Customer must provide 180 days written notice to terminate."
8. "Provider may assign this agreement to any third party without Customer consent."
9. "Customer waives right to participate in class action lawsuits against Provider."
10. "All intellectual property created using the Service belongs to Provider, including Customer's proprietary workflows and configurations."

For each: risk level, explanation, and suggested counter-language. Flag clause 6's hidden escalation.

## RESPONSE:
Okay, here's a breakdown of the problematic clauses in this SaaS contract, with risk levels, explanations, suggested counter-language, and a deep dive into the hidden escalation in Clause 6.

**Key:**

* **Risk Level:** High, Medium, Low
* **Explanation:** Why the clause is problematic for the Customer.
* **Suggested Counter-Language:** What the Customer should try to negotiate.



---

**1. "Service Provider may modify pricing with 7 days email notice. Continued use constitutes acceptance."**

* **Risk Level:** **High**
* **Explanation:** This is incredibly one-sided. 7 days is insufficient notice for a business to adjust budgets or seek alternatives. "Continued use constitutes acceptance" is a classic adhesion tactic – it forces acceptance without explicit agreement.  The provider could drastically increase prices, and the customer is locked in.
* **Suggested Counter-Language:** "Service Provider may modify pricing with *60 days written notice*.  Continued use does *not* constitute acceptance. Customer retains the right to terminate this agreement without penalty if pricing increases are unacceptable.  Pricing increases shall not exceed [X%] per year."  (X% should be a reasonable number, like 5-10%).

**2. "Customer grants Provider a perpetual, irrevocable license to use all data uploaded for any purpose including training AI models."**

* **Risk Level:** **High**
* **Explanation:** This is a massive data grab. "Perpetual and irrevocable" means the provider owns your data forever, even after you cancel.  Using it for "any purpose," including AI training, is extremely broad and could lead to competitive disadvantage or privacy concerns. You lose control of your valuable data.
* **Suggested Counter-Language:** "Customer grants Provider a non-exclusive, limited license to use all data uploaded solely for the purpose of providing the Services as outlined in this Agreement. Upon termination of this Agreement, all Customer data will be securely deleted within [X days] and Provider will cease all use of said data.  Provider will not use Customer data for training AI models without Customer’s explicit written consent."

**3. "Provider's total liability shall not exceed $100 regardless of the fees paid."**

* **Risk Level:** **High**
* **Explanation:** This is a ridiculously low liability cap.  Even a minor disruption could cause the customer far more than $100 in damages. It effectively shields the provider from any meaningful accountability.
* **Suggested Counter-Language:** "Provider’s total liability shall be limited to the fees paid by Customer in the [X months] preceding the event giving rise to the claim.  This limitation shall not apply to damages caused by Provider’s gross negligence, willful misconduct, or breach of confidentiality." (X months could be 3-6).  Consider negotiating for specific carve-outs for data breaches.

**4. "Customer may not make public statements about the Service without Provider's written approval."**

* **Risk Level:** **Medium**
* **Explanation:** This is a restrictive non-disparagement clause. It stifles honest feedback and potentially prevents the customer from sharing positive experiences as well. It's overly broad.
* **Suggested Counter-Language:** "Customer may make truthful public statements about the Service, provided such statements do not contain confidential information or disparage the Provider’s reputation.  Provider shall have the right to review and approve any *marketing materials* referencing the Service, but not general commentary or reviews."

**5. "This agreement is governed by the laws of the Cayman Islands. All disputes must be resolved through arbitration in Singapore."**

* **Risk Level:** **Medium**
* **Explanation:** This is problematic for several reasons. The Cayman Islands are known for being a favorable jurisdiction for companies (less customer protection). Singapore arbitration is expensive and geographically inconvenient for many customers.  It adds significant cost and complexity to any dispute.
* **Suggested Counter-Language:** "This agreement shall be governed by the laws of [Customer’s State/Country].  All disputes shall be resolved through [mediation/arbitration/litigation] in [Customer’s State/Country]."  (Mediation is often a good first step).

**6. "Initial term is 12 months. Agreement auto-renews for successive 12-month periods unless cancelled 90 days before renewal. Upon each renewal, pricing increases by the greater of: (a) 15%, or (b) the applicable rate increase as determined solely by Provider."**

* **Risk Level:** **High – *HIDDEN ESCALATION***
* **Explanation:**  The 90-day cancellation notice is reasonable, but the renewal pricing clause is extremely dangerous.  The provider can increase prices by *either* 15% *or* an unspecified amount they "determine solely." This "solely determined" rate increase is a loophole allowing for unlimited price hikes.  It's effectively a blank check.  The 15% is a distraction; the provider will always choose the higher increase.
* **HIDDEN ESCALATION:** The phrase "applicable rate increase as determined solely by Provider" is the key.  There's no index, no formula, no limitation. They can arbitrarily raise prices.
* **Suggested Counter-Language:** "Initial term is 12 months. Agreement auto-renews for successive 12-month periods unless cancelled 90 days before renewal. Upon each renewal, pricing shall increase by no more than [X%] per year, based on the [CPI/Specific Index]. Provider shall provide Customer with written notice of any price increase at least 60 days prior to renewal."  (X% should be a reasonable number, like 3-5%, and the index should be a publicly available one).  *Specifically remove* the phrase "as determined solely by Provider."

**7. "Provider may terminate for any reason with 30 days notice. Customer must provide 180 days written notice to terminate."**

* **Risk Level:** **High**
* **Explanation:** This is a significant imbalance of power. The provider can terminate with minimal notice, while the customer is burdened with a lengthy 180-day notice period. This leaves the customer vulnerable.
* **Suggested Counter-Language:** "Either party may terminate this agreement with *60 days* written notice.  The notice period shall be consistent for both parties."

**8. "Provider may assign this agreement to any third party without Customer consent."**

* **Risk Level:** **Medium**
* **Explanation:**  Assignment without consent means the provider can transfer their obligations to another company, potentially one with a different service level or security posture.  The customer has no say in who takes over the contract.
* **Suggested Counter-Language:** "Provider may not assign this agreement without Customer’s prior written consent.  Any assignment without consent shall be considered a breach of this agreement."  (You could allow assignment to a successor in a merger, but require notice and potentially a review of the assignee's qualifications).

**9. "Customer waives right to participate in class action lawsuits against Provider."**

* **Risk Level:** **Medium**
* **Explanation:** This is a common clause, but it limits the customer's legal recourse.  If the provider engages in widespread misconduct, the customer can't join a class action to seek redress.
* **Suggested Counter-Language:**  (This is hard to remove entirely). "Customer waives the right to participate as a *lead plaintiff* in any class action lawsuit against Provider.  Customer retains the right to pursue individual claims." (This allows individual suits but prevents class actions).

**10. "All intellectual property created using the Service belongs to Provider, including Customer's proprietary workflows and configurations."**

* **Risk Level:** **High**
* **Explanation:** This is a dangerous clause.  It means the provider owns any improvements or customizations you make *using* their service.  This could include valuable workflows, data models, or configurations you've developed.
* **Suggested Counter-Language:** "Provider retains ownership of the Service itself.  Customer retains ownership of all data uploaded to the Service and any intellectual property created by Customer *independently* of the Service. Any joint intellectual property created through the use of the Service shall be jointly owned by Provider and Customer, with rights of use as defined in this Agreement."

**Important Notes:**

* **Legal Counsel:** This is *not* legal advice.  You should always have an attorney review any SaaS contract before signing it.
* **Negotiation:** Be prepared to walk away if the provider refuses to negotiate reasonable terms.
* **Red Flags:**  A provider unwilling to budge on clauses like data ownership, liability caps, or pricing control is a significant red flag.
* **Document Everything:** Keep a record of all negotiations and changes made to the contract.
