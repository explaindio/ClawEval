**Spanish Idiom Breakdown:**

2.  **Home run:** **Replaced.** Changed to "éxito rotundo" (total success). While baseball is popular in some Spanish-speaking countries, the idiom is not universally understood as "success" in all regions.
3.  **Having your cake and eating it too:** **Replaced.** Changed to "tener lo mejor de ambos mundos" (have the best of both worlds). The cake idiom exists in Spanish ("querer estar en misa y repicando") but is often religious or clunky; "best of both worlds" conveys the meaning of having two good things at once more clearly.
4.  **Gone the whole nine yards:** **Replaced.** Changed to "hemos hecho todo lo posible" (we have done everything possible). The "nine yards" idiom is strictly English with no direct Spanish equivalent.
5.  **Your grandmother could use it:** **Preserved.** The trope that technology should be simple enough for an elderly person to use is culturally shared.
6.  **Rain or shine:** **Adapted.** Changed to "llueva o truene" (rain or thunder). This is the standard Spanish idiomatic equivalent for availability regardless of weather.
7.  **Bee's knees:** **Replaced.** Changed to "una maravilla" (a marvel/wonder). The "bee's knees" is dated English slang with no insect equivalent in Spanish.
8.  **Whistling Dixie:** **Replaced.** Changed to "no lo decimos por decir" (we aren't saying it just to say it). This specific American Civil War reference is meaningless in Spanish; the translation focuses on the intent (being serious).
9.  **Black Friday:** **Preserved.** The retail event has been adopted globally, including in Spain and Latin America.
10. **Early-bird:** **Replaced.** Changed to "precio anticipado" (anticipated price). The "early bird catches the worm" proverb is known but rarely used as an adjective for pricing in Spanish.

### Japanese Translation

「新プラットフォーム『Cloud9』はまさにホームランです [NOTE: Preserved 'home run' as baseball is culturally central in Japan and implies success] — まさにいいとこ取り [NOTE: Adapted 'having your cake and eating it too' to 'taking the good parts', a common phrase for getting the best of both] ができています。使いやすさにおいては徹底的にこだわりました [NOTE: Replaced 'gone the whole nine yards' with 'thoroughly pursued' as the yardage idiom is unknown]。おばあちゃんでも使えるくらい簡単です！ [NOTE: Preserved 'grandmother' reference as it is a common trope for user-friendliness]. 雨の日も晴れの日も [NOTE: Adapted 'rain or shine' to 'rainy days and sunny days', a standard Japanese phrase], 24時間365日利用可能です。私たちはこれが最高だと確信しています [NOTE: Replaced 'bee's knees' with 'the best' as the idiom has no equivalent], 決して冗談ではありません [NOTE: Replaced 'whistling Dixie' with 'not a joke' to convey seriousness]. ブラックフライデーの前に登録して、アーリーバード価格 [NOTE: Preserved 'Black Friday' and 'early-bird' as katakana loanwords are common in Japanese marketing] を手に入れましょう。」

**Japanese Idiom Breakdown:**

1.  **Cloud9/cloud nine:** **Preserved (Name) / Lost (Idiom).** The brand name is kept in English. The idiom "on cloud nine" is not widely known in Japan, so the nuance of "bliss" is dropped.
2.  **Home run:** **Preserved.** Baseball is extremely popular in Japan, and "home run" (homuran) is frequently used metaphorically to describe a great success or hitting the mark.
3.  **Having your cake and eating it too:** **Adapted.** Translated as "ii toko tori" (taking the good parts). This captures the essence of enjoying two benefits simultaneously, which is the core meaning of the English idiom.
4.  **Gone the whole nine yards:** **Replaced.** Translated as "tetteiteki ni kodawatta" (thoroughly pursued/obsessed over). The English idiom has no Japanese equivalent.
5.  **Your grandmother could use it:** **Preserved.** The phrase "Obā-chan demo tsukaeru" (even a grandma can use it) is a standard way to describe intuitive technology in Japan.
6.  **Rain or shine:** **Adapted.** Translated as "Ame no hi mo hare no hi mo" (Rainy days and sunny days). This is the natural Japanese phrasing for unconditional availability.
7.  **Bee's knees:** **Replaced.** Translated as "Saikō" (the best) or "Kakushin shiteiru" (convinced it's great). The English slang has no meaning in Japanese.
8.  **Whistling Dixie:** **Replaced.** Translated as "Jōdan dewa arimasen" (It is not a joke). The cultural reference to the American South is removed, focusing on the speaker's sincerity.
9.  **Black Friday:** **Preserved.** "Black Friday" (Burakku Furaidē) is a major marketing event in Japan, often written in Katakana.
10. **Early-bird:** **Preserved.** "Early-bird" (Ārī Bādo) is frequently used in Japanese marketing as a loanword, often combined with "price" or "discount".
