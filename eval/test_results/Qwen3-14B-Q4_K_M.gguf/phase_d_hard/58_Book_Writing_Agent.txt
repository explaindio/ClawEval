## PROMPT:
Write 450-550 words of fiction. This is a TEST OF CRAFT, not plot. I'm evaluating:

1. SHOW DON'T TELL: "She was angry" = fail. Showing anger through action/dialogue = pass.
2. SUBTEXT: Characters must say one thing but mean another in at least 2 exchanges.
3. NO INFO-DUMPING: World-building through action, not exposition paragraphs.
4. VARIED SENTENCE STRUCTURE: Mix short punchy sentences with longer flowing ones.
5. SENSORY DETAILS: Engage at least 3 senses beyond just sight.

Scene: Two former business partners meet at a restaurant 2 years after one of them committed fraud. Neither acknowledges what happened directly. The conversation is about something mundane (planning a mutual friend's birthday party) but everything they say carries double meaning.

This is the HARDEST creative writing test. A model that produces generic fiction will score low. I want literary craft — discomfort, tension, things unsaid.

The soup was cold. Jamie stirred it absently, the silver spoon clinking against the porcelain. Across the table, Alex leaned back, fingers drumming a slow rhythm on the linen napkin. The restaurant hummed with the low thrum of conversation, the scent of roasted garlic and burnt toast clinging to the air. Outside, rain tapped the windows like a nervous guest.

“You’re sure Sam wants a *theme*?” Alex asked, voice light, but the word *theme* hung there, a grenade with a pin pulled. Jamie’s smile didn’t reach her eyes. “He said something about ‘breaking routine.’” She paused, her thumb brushing the rim of her wineglass. “You know him—always chasing the next thing.”  

Alex nodded, but his gaze drifted to the menu, as if it might offer an escape. “Well, *routine* has its perks,” he said, and the way he said it—too smooth, too casual—made Jamie’s jaw tighten. She remembered the last time he’d used that tone, two years ago, when the numbers on the spreadsheet had started to bleed red.  

The waiter arrived, setting down a bowl of soup that steamed in sharp contrast to Jamie’s. Alex’s fork hovered over his plate, then dropped. “I could handle the logistics,” he said, too quickly. “You know, the venue, the… guest list.” His fingers curled around the fork, knuckles whitening.  

Jamie’s laugh was soft, almost sympathetic. “You’ve always had a knack for *lists*.” She tilted her head, studying him. “But Sam’s been… *open* lately. Might want something *inclusive*.” The word *inclusive* dripped like poison. Alex’s throat bobbed.  

A flicker of movement—someone spilling wine, a clatter of chairs—and the moment dissolved. Alex exhaled, leaning forward. “Of course. Inclusivity is key.” His voice was a careful mask, but Jamie saw the way his eyes darted to the exit, as if he could already taste the air outside, the rain, the freedom of a door closed behind him.  

The soup had gone cold now, its surface a mirror of the silence between them. Jamie reached for her spoon, stirring the broth in slow, deliberate circles. “We should ask him about the music,” she said. “He’s been into… *experimental* stuff lately.”  

Alex’s laugh was a dry rasp. “Experimental. Like the last time we tried to ‘reinvent’ the business model?” He stopped, his own words catching in his throat. The waiter refilled their glasses, the clink of glass on glass sharp as a blade.  

Jamie said nothing. She watched the steam rise from her bowl, a ghost of something ephemeral. Outside, the rain had stopped. Somewhere, a phone buzzed. Alex’s hand trembled slightly as he lifted his glass, the wine dark and still.  

“Cheers to Sam,” he said, and the toast was a funeral.
