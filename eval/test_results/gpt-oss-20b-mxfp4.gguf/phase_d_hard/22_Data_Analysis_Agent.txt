## PROMPT:
Analyze this clinical trial data. WARNING: The obvious conclusion from the aggregate data is WRONG. You must analyze the subgroups to find the truth. This is a real statistical phenomenon.

Overall Results:
- Treatment A: 100 patients, 40 recovered (40%)
- Treatment B: 100 patients, 50 recovered (50%)

Subgroup breakdown:
- Mild cases with Treatment A: 10 patients, 8 recovered (80%)
- Mild cases with Treatment B: 60 patients, 45 recovered (75%)
- Severe cases with Treatment A: 90 patients, 32 recovered (35.6%)
- Severe cases with Treatment B: 40 patients, 5 recovered (12.5%)

Questions:
1. Based on aggregate data alone, which treatment appears better?
2. When you look at subgroups, which treatment is actually better for mild cases?
3. Which treatment is actually better for severe cases?
4. Explain the paradox — why does the aggregate data mislead?
5. What is the name of this statistical phenomenon?
6. What is your final recommendation and why?

## RESPONSE:
**1.  Aggregate view**

| Treatment | Patients | Recovered | Recovery % |
|-----------|----------|-----------|------------|
| A | 100 | 40 | **40 %** |
| B | 100 | 50 | **50 %** |

*On the surface, Treatment B looks better.*

---

**2.  Mild‑case subgroup**

| Treatment | Mild patients | Recovered | Recovery % |
|-----------|---------------|-----------|------------|
| A | 10 | 8 | **80 %** |
| B | 60 | 45 | **75 %** |

*For mild disease, Treatment A actually has the higher recovery rate.*

---

**3.  Severe‑case subgroup**

| Treatment | Severe patients | Recovered | Recovery % |
|-----------|-----------------|-----------|------------|
| A | 90 | 32 | **35.6 %** |
| B | 40 | 5 | **12.5 %** |

*For severe disease, Treatment A is again superior.*

---

**4.  Why the aggregate data misleads**

The two treatments are **not balanced** with respect to disease severity:

- **Treatment A**: 10 mild / 90 severe  
- **Treatment B**: 60 mild / 40 severe  

Because mild patients recover at a much higher rate than severe patients, the overall recovery rate for Treatment B is inflated by the large number of mild cases it contains. When you pool the data, the “good” mild cases in the B group pull the overall percentage up, masking the fact that B performs poorly in both subgroups.

This is a classic example of **Simpson’s paradox** (also called the Yule–Simpson effect). A confounding variable (here, severity) creates a spurious association that reverses when the data are stratified.

---

**5.  Statistical phenomenon**

**Simpson’s paradox** – an association that appears in aggregated data but disappears or reverses when the data are partitioned by a confounding factor.

---

**6.  Final recommendation**

- **Choose Treatment A.**  
  - It yields a higher recovery rate in *both* mild and severe subgroups.  
  - The apparent advantage of Treatment B in the aggregate is an artifact of the uneven distribution of severity.  
  - A proper analysis (stratified or adjusted for severity) shows A’s superiority.

**Practical take‑away:**  
Always examine subgroup or stratified results, especially when the treatment groups differ in key prognostic variables. Relying solely on aggregate figures can lead to the wrong clinical decision.
