### Explanation of Adaptations

**1. "Cloud9" / "cloud nine"**
*   **Spanish/Japanese:** **Preserved (Name) / Lost (Pun).** The brand name "Cloud9" was kept as a proper noun. However, the double meaning of the idiom "on cloud nine" (feeling euphoric) does not translate directly into the brand name in either language, so the pun was lost.

**2. "Home run"**
*   **Spanish:** **Replaced.** Baseball is not a dominant sport in most Spanish-speaking countries. I replaced it with "éxito total" (total success).
*   **Japanese:** **Replaced.** While baseball is popular in Japan, "home run" is not used metaphorically to describe a product's success in business contexts. I replaced it with "大当たり" (big hit/jackpot).

**3. "Having your cake and eating it too"**
*   **Spanish:** **Adapted.** The literal translation is confusing. I adapted it to "lo mejor de ambos mundos" (the best of both worlds), which conveys the same desire for two incompatible benefits.
*   **Japanese:** **Adapted.** I used "一石二鳥" (two birds, one stone). While the directionality is different (gaining two things vs keeping one and using it), it is the standard cultural equivalent for gaining double benefits without compromise.

**4. "Gone the whole nine yards"**
*   **Spanish:** **Replaced.** This idiom is unknown. I replaced it with "hemos hecho todo lo posible" (we have done everything possible).
*   **Japanese:** **Replaced.** I replaced it with "あらゆる手を尽くしました" (took every possible measure), which focuses on the thoroughness of the effort.

**5. "Even your grandmother could use it"**
*   **Spanish:** **Preserved.** The stereotype of older generations struggling with technology is universal in the West. The translation remained literal.
*   **Japanese:** **Adapted.** I changed "your grandmother" to "お年寄り" (the elderly/older people). Referring specifically to "your grandmother" in a marketing context can sound slightly intrusive or overly familiar in Japanese, so a generalized term is more polite and effective.

**6. "Rain or shine"**
*   **Spanish:** **Replaced.** I used "pase lo que pase" (whatever happens), which is the standard way to express unconditional availability.
*   **Japanese:** **Preserved.** The phrase "雨の日も晴れの日も" (rainy days and sunny days) is a perfectly natural Japanese idiom used frequently in service promises.

**7. "The bee's knees"**
*   **Spanish:** **Replaced.** This idiom is archaic and untranslatable literally. I replaced it with "una maravilla" (a marvel/wonder).
*   **Japanese:** **Replaced.** I replaced it with "最高" (the best/saikou), which is the standard enthusiastic descriptor for products.

**8. "Whistling Dixie"**
*   **Spanish:** **Replaced.** This is a highly specific American cultural reference. I replaced it with "no es solo palabrería" (it is not just empty words/waffle).
*   **Japanese:** **Replaced.** I replaced it with "大げさな話ではありません" (it is not an exaggeration), fitting the context of verifying a claim.

**9. "Black Friday"**
*   **Spanish:** **Preserved.** "Viernes Negro" is now a standard retail term in many Spanish-speaking countries.
*   **Japanese:** **Preserved.** "ブラックフライデー" is widely recognized in Japan as a major sales event.

**10. "Early-bird"**
*   **Spanish:** **Replaced.** The bird metaphor is not used here. I replaced it with "precio especial por reserva anticipada" (special price for advance booking).
*   **Japanese:** **Replaced.** I used the compound word "早割" (hayawari), which is the standard industry term for "early-bird discount."
