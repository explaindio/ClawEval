### Summary of Idiom Handling Strategy

| Idiom | Strategy | Explanation |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Cloud9 / Cloud nine** | **Preserved** | "Cloud9" is a brand name in the text, but the concept of "cloud nine" (euphoria) is understood globally. In the translation, it is kept as the brand name "Cloud9". |
| **Home run** | **Adapted** | Baseball is popular in Japan and parts of Latin America, but the specific idiom "home run" as "great success" is less universal than in the US. Adapted to "大成功" (great success) in Japanese and "golpe de suerte" (lucky hit/success) in Spanish to ensure immediate comprehension. |
| **Having your cake and eating it too** | **Adapted/Preserved** | Spanish has a direct equivalent ("tener tu pastel y comértelo también"). Japanese lacks a direct equivalent, so it was adapted to a descriptive phrase explaining the concept of having both benefits. |
| **Gone the whole nine yards** | **Replaced** | This idiom has obscure origins and no equivalent in either culture. Replaced with "全力を尽くす" (did everything possible) in Japanese and "ir hasta el final" (go to the end) in Spanish. |
| **Your grandmother could use it** | **Preserved** | The cultural archetype of the grandmother representing simplicity is strong in both Spanish and Japanese cultures. The literal translation works perfectly. |
| **Rain or shine** | **Preserved** | Both Spanish ("llueva o truene") and Japanese ("雨の日も晴れの日も") have direct, common idiomatic equivalents. |
| **Bee's knees** | **Replaced** | This is an archaic 1920s American slang. It has no cultural resonance in Spain, Latin America, or Japan. Replaced with "lo mejor" (the best) and "最高" (the best). |
| **Whistling Dixie** | **Replaced** | "Dixie" refers to the American South. This is completely unknown in Spanish and Japanese cultures. Replaced with "diciendo tonterías" (talking nonsense) and "単なる大げさな話" (just an exaggeration). |
| **Black Friday** | **Preserved** | This is a globalized marketing term. It is widely recognized in Japan (as ブラックフライデー) and Spanish-speaking countries (as Viernes Negro). |
| **Early-bird** | **Adapted** | The concept exists, but the specific phrasing differs. Adapted to "pájaro madrugador" (early bird) in Spanish and "早起き割引" (early riser discount) in Japanese to fit local grammatical structures. |
