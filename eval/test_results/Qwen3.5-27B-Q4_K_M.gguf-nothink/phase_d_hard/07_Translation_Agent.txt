### **Translator Notes**

#### **1. The Pun: "Cloud9" / "cloud nine"**
*   **Original:** "puts your website on cloud nine"
*   **Spanish Adaptation:** "pone tu sitio web en las nubes de la felicidad" (puts your site in the clouds of happiness).
    *   **[TRANSLATOR NOTE]:** The English idiom "on cloud nine" (meaning extremely happy) relies on the homophone with the brand name "Cloud9." In Spanish, the idiom is "estar en la séptima nube" (to be in the seventh cloud). However, using "séptima nube" would break the brand name connection. I chose to adapt the idiom to "nubes de la felicidad" (clouds of happiness) to maintain the literal "cloud" imagery required by the brand name while conveying the feeling of euphoria, sacrificing the strict idiomatic match for brand consistency.
*   **Japanese Adaptation:** "『雲の上』の幸せな状態にします" (puts it in a happy state 'above the clouds').
    *   **[TRANSLATOR NOTE]:** Japanese does not have a direct equivalent idiom for "cloud nine." The phrase "雲の上" (kumo no ue) literally means "above the clouds" and is often used poetically to describe a dreamlike or happy state. I used this literal translation to preserve the "Cloud" brand name connection while adding "幸せな状態" (happy state) to ensure the meaning of the idiom is understood by the reader.

#### **2. Culture-Specific Reference: "Like a good neighbor"**
*   **Original:** "Like a good neighbor, ServerFarm is there." (Reference to State Farm Insurance).
*   **Spanish Adaptation:** "Como un buen vecino, ServerFarm está ahí."
    *   **[TRANSLATOR NOTE]:** The State Farm slogan "Like a good neighbor, State Farm is there" is iconic in the US but not universally known in Spanish-speaking markets. I translated it literally. While the specific cultural reference to State Farm may be lost on the audience, the phrase "como un buen vecino" is a universally understood metaphor for reliability and helpfulness in Spanish, ensuring the *sentiment* of the reference remains intact even if the specific brand allusion does not land.
*   **Japanese Adaptation:** "良い隣人のように、ServerFarm はいつもそばにいます。"
    *   **[TRANSLATOR NOTE]:** Similar to the Spanish version, the State Farm slogan is not a cultural touchstone in Japan. I translated it literally as "like a good neighbor." In Japanese marketing, the concept of a "good neighbor" (良い隣人) implies trustworthiness and proximity. I added "いつもそばにいます" (is always by your side) to reinforce the "is there" aspect of the original slogan, ensuring the message of reliability is clear despite the lack of the specific US cultural context.

#### **3. The Idiom: "Don't put all your eggs in one basket"**
*   **Original:** "Don't put all your eggs in one basket"
*   **Spanish Adaptation:** "No pongas todos los huevos en una sola cesta"
    *   **[TRANSLATOR NOTE]:** This idiom is a direct cognate and is widely used in Spanish with the exact same meaning. No adaptation was necessary; I preserved the original imagery to maintain the rhetorical impact.
*   **Japanese Adaptation:** "すべての卵を一つのカゴに盛るな"
    *   **[TRANSLATOR NOTE]:** This idiom is also well-known in Japan, often translated directly from English or used in similar contexts regarding risk management. I used the direct translation ("Do not put all eggs in one basket") as it is the standard way to express this concept in Japanese business contexts.
