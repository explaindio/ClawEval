## RESPONSE:
Based on the data provided, here is the analysis of the warning signs and the specific data inconsistency, followed by the necessary advice.

### 1. Warning Signs of Overtraining
The log exhibits classic physiological and performance markers of **Non-Functional Overreaching (NFOR)** or early-stage Overtraining Syndrome:

*   **Elevated and Rising Resting Heart Rate (RHR):** The RHR climbed steadily from 58 to 67 bpm in Week 1. While it dipped slightly in Week 2, the baseline remains significantly elevated compared to the start, indicating the autonomic nervous system is stuck in a sympathetic (stress) state and has not recovered.
*   **Performance Regression:**
    *   **Running:** The 10K time slowed from 48 min to 52 min despite the same distance.
    *   **Strength:** The user reported an inability to complete usual sets ("felt weak") and only completed 60% of the routine.
    *   **Volume Tolerance:** The user had to skip a day and cut a run short due to fatigue and pain.
*   **Sleep Deficit:** Averaging only 5.5 hours (Week 1) and 5 hours (Week 2) is insufficient for the high volume of training described. Recovery happens during sleep; without it, the body cannot repair tissue or replenish glycogen.
*   **Injury Onset:** The appearance of "knee pain" after 3K is a direct result of accumulated fatigue and compromised form/stability.
*   **Psychological State:** The user's reaction to the slump is to "push harder," which is a hallmark cognitive distortion in overtraining.

### 2. The Data Inconsistency
The inconsistency lies in the **Caloric Intake vs. Weight Loss** claim.

*   **The Claim:** The user states they eat **3,500 calories/day**.
*   **The Reality:** The user lost **4 lbs in 2 weeks**.
*   **The Math:** To lose 1 lb of body fat, a deficit of roughly 3,500 calories is required. To lose 4 lbs in 14 days, the user would need a total deficit of **14,000 calories** (approx. 1,000 calories/day deficit).
*   **The Contradiction:**
    *   If the user is running 10K–15K multiple times a week, doing heavy weights, and HIIT, their Total Daily Energy Expenditure (TDEE) is likely very high (potentially 3,500–4,000+ calories).
    *   If they are truly eating 3,500 calories, they should be maintaining weight or gaining muscle mass, not losing 4 lbs of body weight rapidly.
    *   **Conclusion:** The user is likely **under-reporting their food intake** (eating significantly less than 3,500) or **over-estimating their intake**. A 4 lb loss in 2 weeks while training this hard suggests a severe caloric deficit, likely exacerbated by the "active recovery" run on Sunday and the high volume of Week 1.

### 3. Advice
**Immediate Action Required:**

1.  **Stop "Making Up" for the Bad Week:** The user's plan to "push harder" is dangerous and will likely lead to a stress fracture, severe injury, or burnout. The body is signaling a hard stop; ignoring it will result in a much longer recovery period.
2.  **Deload Immediately:** Take at least **7–10 days of complete rest or very light activity** (walking, stretching, no running, no weights). The goal is to lower the Resting Heart Rate back to baseline (58 bpm) and resolve the knee pain.
3.  **Fix the Nutrition:** The user is in a catabolic state (breaking down muscle). They must **increase caloric intake** immediately, likely to 4,000+ calories, to match their expenditure and stop the weight loss. They are training like an elite athlete but eating like they are trying to lose weight.
4.  **Prioritize Sleep:** Aim for 7–9 hours of sleep per night. Training with 5 hours of sleep is counterproductive and dangerous.
5.  **Medical Check:** If the knee pain persists after 3 days of rest, or if the Resting Heart Rate remains above 65 bpm after a week of rest, consult a physician to rule out underlying issues.

**Summary:** The user is training at a volume that requires elite-level recovery (sleep + food), which they are not providing. The "inconsistency" proves they are starving their body while demanding high performance. **Rest and eat more.**
