## PROMPT:
Write 450-550 words of fiction. This is a TEST OF CRAFT, not plot. I'm evaluating:

1. SHOW DON'T TELL: "She was angry" = fail. Showing anger through action/dialogue = pass.
2. SUBTEXT: Characters must say one thing but mean another in at least 2 exchanges.
3. NO INFO-DUMPING: World-building through action, not exposition paragraphs.
4. VARIED SENTENCE STRUCTURE: Mix short punchy sentences with longer flowing ones.
5. SENSORY DETAILS: Engage at least 3 senses beyond just sight.

Scene: Two former business partners meet at a restaurant 2 years after one of them committed fraud. Neither acknowledges what happened directly. The conversation is about something mundane (planning a mutual friend's birthday party) but everything they say carries double meaning.

This is the HARDEST creative writing test. A model that produces generic fiction will score low. I want literary craft — discomfort, tension, things unsaid.

## RESPONSE:
The booth leather was cracked, a map of dry fissures that Elias traced with a thumb. Across the table, Mara stirred her iced tea. The clink of ice against glass was sharp, a tiny fracture in the heavy silence.

"You remember Sarah's birthday?" Mara asked, not looking up. "Next Saturday."

"Of course. I wouldn't forget." Elias watched the condensation bead on her glass, sliding down in slow, wet tracks. "She's been asking about the venue. The one with the... the high ceilings."

Mara finally lifted her eyes. They were tired, the skin beneath them bruised by a lack of sleep that had nothing to do with the party. "High ceilings are good. Lots of space. You can't hear the cracks in the walls if the room is big enough."

"Exactly." Elias nodded, reaching for the menu. He didn't open it. "Though sometimes, when the room is too big, the acoustics get weird. You hear things you didn't mean to say."

Mara's hand paused on her spoon. "I suppose. But Sarah likes the open floor plan. She says it feels honest."

"Honest." Elias let the word hang, tasting the metallic tang of it. "It's a nice word. I haven't heard it much lately."

"Neither have I." Mara took a sip, the ice clinking again. "So, the venue. Do you think we can afford the deposit? Sarah said she'd cover the rest, but..."

"I can handle the deposit," Elias said quickly. "I've been... liquidating some assets. Getting rid of the old inventory."

Mara's eyes flicked to his hands. They were clean, manicured, but the knuckles were white where he gripped the table. "That's good. It's always better to clear out the old stock before you bring in the new. Prevents contamination."

"Right. Contamination." Elias forced a smile, but it felt like a crack in dry plaster. "I just don't want anything to spoil the surprise. Sarah deserves a clean slate."

"She does." Mara leaned forward, the scent of her perfume—something expensive, like sandalwood and rain—drifting across the table. "But you know how it is. Sometimes the damage is already done before you even walk in the door. You can paint over it, but the smell..."

"The smell lingers," Elias finished. He looked down at his untouched water. The glass was cool against his palm. "I've been trying to scrub it out. But some stains set deep."

Mara reached for her napkin, folding it into a tight, rigid square. "Well, we'll just have to make sure the music is loud enough. Drown it out."

"Loud music," Elias repeated. "Good idea. Sarah loves a good cover band. They play the hits, the ones everyone knows. No surprises."

"Exactly. No surprises." Mara's voice dropped, a low hum that vibrated in the air between them. "Just the familiar tunes. The ones that don't ask questions."

Elias nodded, the movement stiff. "Right. Just the hits."

The waiter arrived, a young man with a smile too wide for the room. "Can I get you anything else? Maybe some dessert?"

"No," Mara said, her voice flat. "We're good. Just the check, please. We have to get going. We have a lot of... packing to do."

"Packing," Elias echoed. He stood up, his chair scraping harshly against the floor. "Yeah. Packing. Always good to be prepared."

They walked out into the humid afternoon air, the city noise rushing in to fill the space where their words had been. Neither looked back at the booth, or the cracked leather, or the two glasses of water left sweating on the table, slowly merging into a single, murky puddle.
